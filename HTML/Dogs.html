Learn about dog adoption, nutrition, exercise, and grooming.

Introduction to Canine Domestication

The domestic dog is a highly social species, with over 300 breeds recognized globally by the World Canine Organization. The relationship between dogs and their owners is mutually beneficial, providing substantial social and health advantages. Numerous studies have confirmed that owning a dog can reduce feelings of loneliness and promote physical health, including lowered blood pressure and improved lipid profiles.

Lifecycle Nutrition and Exercise

Dietary Management Across Life Stages:
  • Puppies: Puppies should nurse for the first two months before transitioning to solid meals. Feed them three times a day from 3 to 6 months, then gradually reduce to two meals a day.
  • Hydration: Dogs' bodies are composed of 60-70% water. Dehydration can be dangerous, so always ensure continuous access to clean water.
Exercise Recommendations:
  • Activity Levels: Dogs need regular exercise to stay healthy. The amount of exercise varies by breed, but 30 to 120 minutes of activity per day is typical.

Grooming and Handling

Routine Grooming:
  • Brushing: Regular brushing is essential to manage shedding and prevent matting. Check for ticks and fleas after outdoor activities.
  • Bathing: Use dog-appropriate shampoo to avoid skin irritation. Rinse thoroughly to remove all soap residue.
Safe Handling Techniques:
  • Small Dogs: Support small dogs by placing one hand under their chest and the other supporting their rear.
  • Large Dogs: When lifting, use both arms to support the chest and rear for stability.

Frequently Asked Questions

To eliminate fleas, use a flea-specific shampoo and follow the instructions carefully. If irritation occurs, stop use and consult a veterinarian immediately.

Feed high-quality commercial dog food that is appropriate for your dog's age, size, and activity level. Avoid human foods that can be harmful, such as chocolate and onions.

Check your dog's weight by feeling for their ribs. If you can't easily feel their ribs, they may be overweight. For more precise information, consult your veterinarian.
